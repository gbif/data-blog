Biodiversity hotspots and GBIF data

John Waller
2019-09-10
GBIF

GBIF organizes and gives statistics for countries and areas, but we do not do a good job of reporting on occurrence data for other areas that might be more biologically relevant or important. Currently the Critical Ecosystem Partnership Fund (CEPF) recognizes 35 biodiversity hotspots.

For a region to be considered a hotspot it must:

  • Contain at least 1,500 endemic species of vascular plants (found nowhere else on Earth).
  • Have lost at least 70 percent of its primary native vegetation.

A biodiversity hotspot by this definition is a region that is diverse and under threat.

Using vascular plants as a proxy for total biodiversity is reasonable, because:

  1. Vascular plants are a well-studied group >200 M occurrences (only birds have more)
  2. Most of the diversity of a region will correlate with its plant diversity

Below I plot 35 biodiversity hotspots I obtained from this hotspot shapefile here. I calculate some simple statistics to judge how well the GBIF network is doing at collecting data from these areas.

  • Total Count: the total number of occurrences records (with coordinates) on GBIF.
  • Record Density (on land): number of occurrences / land area of hotspot
  • Non-bird Density (on land): number of not-bird occurrences / land area of hotspot
  • Record Density (with water): number of occurrences / total area of hotspot including water
